Imagine the internet as a vast, intricate city where data is the currency, and fibre optic cables are the golden roads that connect its inhabitants. As you type, a message is sent via Morse code to the local post office – your router – which forwards it to the global sorting office – a network of servers – that dispatches it to its final destination, often traversing the world in a fraction of a second.

In reality, packets of data are fragmented into tiny little packets of Lego bricks, stamped with an address label, and then sent out into cyber space, where they hop from router to router like digital courier services, eventually reassembling into the original message you're trying to send.

It starts with a single, sparkling key press on your keyboard, sending an electromagnetic signal dancing along the fibre optic cables like a flash of light, until it reaches the data centres, where an army of humming servers and whirring hard drives convert them into a digital signal that's broadcast to the world.
