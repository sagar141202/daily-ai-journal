In 18th-century Japan, a bizarre phenomenon occurred: the 'Floating World' (Ukiyo-e) of woodblock prints, which were meant to be destroyed after one viewing. Artists like Hokusai and Hiroshige produced these enchanting pieces, often featuring scenes of landscapes, women, and travelers. But here's the intriguing part: these prints were actually designed to be ephemeral, like a 'ghostly whisper,' meant to vanish into thin air as they fulfilled their brief purpose, only to be reborn in the imagination of the viewer. Little wonder that the art form has endured for centuries.
